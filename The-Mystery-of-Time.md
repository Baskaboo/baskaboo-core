## Four Questions Science Cannot Answer

Physics can measure time with extraordinary precision — atomic clocks, relativistic corrections, GPS satellites. But it cannot answer four fundamental questions:

**1. Why does time flow only forward?**
The equations of physics — quantum mechanics, relativity, even thermodynamics at the microscopic level — are symmetrical with respect to time. They work identically whether time runs forward or backward. And yet we experience an unmistakable direction. This is called "the arrow of time" and remains unsolved.

**2. What is "now"?**
The theory of relativity tells us that "now" is not absolute — two observers moving at different speeds have different "nows." And yet we each experience a single, unambiguous present moment. Why?

**3. Why does time exist at all?**
In quantum gravity (the Wheeler-DeWitt equation), time disappears entirely from the equations — the equation of the universe contains no *t*. This is called "the problem of time" and is among the deepest open problems in theoretical physics.

**4. Why is there no time in the quantum layer?**
When physicists write the equation of the entire universe in quantum gravity, the variable *t* disappears. Time is simply not there. This has troubled physicists for sixty years — and no one has explained why a complete description of reality requires no time at all.

## Falsification Anchors

Two experimental paths open from this framework. They have different levels of readiness, and honesty requires stating which is which.

### Experiment 1 — Cosmic Light Dispersion

If Level A space is composed of microscopic φ-spiral pixels (3φ), then light traveling billions of years should encounter them. Because blue light corresponds to the 1st φ-step (Energy→Matter) and red light to the 2nd and 3rd φ-steps (Space/Time), the two colors should arrive with an infinitesimally small time difference whose harmonic coefficient contains φ.

**Important caveat:** This experiment already exists. It is called Lorentz Invariance Violation (LIV) testing and has been performed with the Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope since 2008 — measuring exactly whether different frequencies of light from gamma-ray bursts (billions of light-years away) arrive with a time difference. The current result: **no signal detected**. The limits are extremely tight — any delay must be smaller than 10⁻¹⁹ times the Planck time.

This does not automatically falsify Baskaboo — but it means that if the framework predicts a difference, it must predict a **specific, extremely small value** that is consistent with these limits. Without that specific value, the experiment cannot falsify anything. This remains an open quantitative task.

### Experiment 2 — Quantum Oscillations in MIT Quasicrystals

If the quasicrystal carries the φ-relation in its structure, it should produce quantum oscillations with a characteristic frequency corresponding to the φ-deficit — the same remainder that Baskaboo identifies as the source of time.

This is the more immediately testable prediction, and it connects directly to the quasicrystal investigation in progress. Unlike Experiment 1, it does not conflict with existing data — it proposes a new measurement in a system (electronic quasicrystals, MIT 2025) that has not yet been examined for this specific signature.

**What confirmation looks like:** Detection of quantum oscillations in bilayer quasicrystal systems whose frequency ratio converges to φ across multiple experimental configurations.

**What falsification looks like:** Oscillations present but with no φ-correlated structure, or absence of oscillations entirely in the predicted energy window.

1. **Smooth spacetime:** If it is proven that Level A spacetime is perfectly continuous (smooth) and does not consist of discrete frames or information pixels, the three-step rendering architecture collapses entirely.

The second criterion is particularly strong: it is clear, testable, and cannot be adjusted post-hoc. No such system has been found. Until one is, the framework survives.
